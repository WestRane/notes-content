## A hidden agricultural gem
*June 2026*

The story kicks off with a deceptively simple exposition: the boy relocates to some agricultural high school. I wouldn't lie if I said it's fairly unusual to see such a theme being present, let alone being the driving force of the story. The writers aren't willing to keep their cards hidden - or so it seems - and they throw the viewer into the stables, revealing the true side of chicken eggs and pork.

There is one particular thing that most slice-of-life-laden stories are struggling with: it's writing a good number of reasons for viewers to actually commit to what authors have to say. As the genre itself dictates a slow pace, an obvious lack of endless fights and grand speeches, the writers are forced to use 200% of their brain potential to please all sorts of audiences.

Did the authors of Gin no Saji actually succeed in this relatively tough task? Yeah, pretty much.

### Practical use of animals and its drama

It's mostly safe to say Pork Bowl made the story a hundred times more engaging. Yeah, one simple pig. Not a flying nor speaking one, a simple pig.

The reason for this occurrence is a peculiar one too... it's drama without a single tear shed. Most characters either tell Hachiken to get used to it or make fair attempts to use logic to justify these somewhat cruel acts of violence toward the pigs.

Moreover, it seems somewhat unbelievable, but even at the end the authors don't push veganism down our throats. Furthermore, many characters actually admit that meat and dairy are what we basically need to survive. The grumblings of those who care about pigs and cows and value their life at its fullest are fading amidst people yelling "It's so yummy!". It doesn't make this series right-wing or something, but the absence of obvious lectures to the viewer feels nice, and the actual problem and moral difficulty is still held well.

The actual debates of whether we should eat meat or not aren't the primary area of concern. We don't see people trying to replace dairy, nor do we see the characters being actually mean toward the animals. The equestrian club members genuinely love their horses and care about them, even if it means eventually letting them go if they weren't to succeed in a harsh race. Meanwhile the classmates of Hachiken, when it comes to their family businesses, treat pigs, cows and chickens differently. Tamako uses modern technologies while the other students' families have a more classic farm approach, which allows them to care about animals in the way nobody else does.

On one hand, it seems shocking to use the cow's milk that was supposed to belong to her offspring. With that in mind, Tamako still lets the calf drink her mother's milk, even if it's for the calf's being healthy in the future. What we see here is on the other hand people actually care for their animals, because they love them, while also making profit for survival.

### Growth of Hachiken

What's worth noting is that none of this profit is made out of nowhere. People invest in their livestock, both literally and figuratively. Komaba's mother spends many hours every day caring for their cows and other animals, which only leads to a small profit. Meanwhile the animals there are treated genuinely nicely.

Ichirou Komaba's story isn't relatively complex, but it's simply realistic. This characteristic is probably the best out there for the story of such a genre. Characters help each other and grow up together, even if it means the authors somewhat force them to scold at each other. Yeah, the quarrel between Hachiken and Komaba definitely was a little forced, but it developed the reasoning of yet another character's struggle.

Take a look at Hachiken himself. He came to the agricultural school with horrific pride of being "my classmates are superior when it comes to dairy sciences, but I'm still better at basic school knowledge" only to get absolutely destroyed on that matter. With all that happening around, it's no wonder his complexes revealed even more.

These two matters basically show the other side of the coin: you might think you're smart - believing your marks on general subjects are better - but the agricultural school will stomp on you until you realize you're not as smart as you carry yourself. Such a cruel reality leads Hachiken to his deep complex of "everyone has a dream and they simply move toward it, while I'm just a commoner without any idea of what the future holds for me". But the presence of Komaba and many other people lets Hachiken realize that this "move toward it" is actually an obnoxiously hard and complicated process.

The show depicts an absolutely grounded way of making a teenager - or even an adult - accept true reality and shape a healthy way of thinking. Hachiken's pride is nowhere to be found, but his complexes regarding his lack of plans for the future isn't the case at the end of the story as much as it was before. He finds that golden spot - the middle ground between deep reflections and total lack of self-awareness, which is absolutely marvelous to watch.

### Conclusion

Like in many other animes, of course the issues are present too. The UFO-ish themed episode is hilariously boring. Its setup is so long. The details of Hachiken's parents are nowhere to be seen (at least for now, hopefully). Mikage's progression is basically letting some of her concerns slip out of her tongue. She acts naturally when dealing with main character's questions, but the absence of the actual development is still undeniable.

Gin no Saji steps on to a pretty niche genre of showing animals being treated both cutely and harshly, being killed and cared for. The moral dilemma of the story is actually engaging, while the characters make natural and grounded conclusions, developing their profiles further. Moreover, there are so many details regarding dairy culture, livestock treatment and so on, it feels like finding a hidden gem - despite being old nowadays - for those who've never seen a pig in their life. And it's funny too - Hachiken comparing his "loss amongst his peers" with the same loss of Pork Bowl is both hilarious and actually so realistic for him to say.

The show - despite its slice-of-life core - doesn't feel tedious. This is indeed a refreshing experience.
