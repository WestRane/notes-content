## Lack of balance between power and immaturity
*January 2026*

I'm genuinely convinced it's hardly necessary to write critiques of shoujo anime because of its general tropes. They are well known, and I'm not exactly the target audience of the genre itself, so to speak. But "Silent Witch" is quite uncommon — not only does it have good production, visuals and so forth, it also tries to depict some harsh stories. It's not the only reason I've watched it, but nevertheless, it's one of the main ones. Truth be told, those gritty stories made the anime juvenile, contrary to how you'd expect such stories to influence the viewing experience. That's why I feel like I should probably express my thoughts.

### The Bright Side

Before actually proceeding to the stuff that managed to strike me as obnoxious, let's dive into the... other parts. Or rather touch on them briefly. The main heroine is cute. The way the maid talks to her master Louis is endearing and makes me laugh every single time. Oh, and it's pretty interesting to see the writers make some characters maintain their sort of mythical backstory. It's mostly applicable to the prince. Like I said previously, the visuals here are great, the good music is literally the reason I encountered this story. Also I really appreciate the details, consider the "nameless paper under the tea" or "corset+makeup making the heroine regain her composure". They may sound random, but I find them unexpectedly clever here.

### The Collapse of Character Logic

Conversely, there is one specific scene that managed to make me incredibly infuriated. The scene where Monica ended up throwing the entirety of potential of her character development down the drain. The "save Casey" scene. What's wrong with that, you might ask me?

If the premise is to establish some drama and make the viewers cry, then the author should make it grounded. He could write such a story with a big attention to details, and I would label it with some sophisticated wording and be happy. If the author decides not to make drama at all, it's up to him. But when the writer force-feeds me with some truly immature actions within the harsh narrative, that means I have no choice but to criticize it.

Casey is a terrorist. Let's make it as clear as possible: she did her best not only to kill the prince, but inevitably to wound many other students, Monica's friends included. She basically lied to Monica multiple times in countless ways to succeed in her murder. I'm glad this action has some motivation behind it, thus making it at least somewhat reasonable. Of course we barely know about the prince's actual motives to become a king etc, but it's pretty hard to fit such details in a 13-episode anime. But the problem is that we see Monica trying to defend Casey!

Our main heroine is so kind, sweet, warm and stuff that she shows sympathy towards the person that tried to kill her, the prince and potentially her friends. Moreover, to the person that was faking their entire friendship. That scene could've resulted in the other way. Imagine this: Monica struggles with her social anxiety, tries her best to adapt for almost 10 episodes, and when a tough situation happens, she SHOWS us her progress. She could've done a fair and reasonable judgement towards Casey despite being in tears. I could've clapped and cried. Especially considering it's not the first time she loses a friend. Instead, she acts as if Casey stole a candy and is now sentenced to death.

Ultimately, this is the main absurd thing that happens in this story. Monica is being presented as an immature, morally blind character that leads the potentially strong stories into the sweet, yet incredibly stupid outcomes. She remains to be as sweet, yet as weak, as immature despite any events happening around her. She literally has a job of preventing assaults (!) on the prince, but she is just overpowered in her magic skills, and that's it. She continues to be the very same person. The only thing that changes is that she can now say "I'm Monica, nice to meet you" and "I won't forgive you!" to her truly cruel and stupid ex-friend within the forced drama. And even then, she saves him, continuing on being weak and... boring.

And it's totally fine to be mostly the same person in a shoujo anime when the only stuff that happens around you is an environment change, new friends appearing in your life and such. But when you have social anxiety, you're a powerful mage with big responsibilities, when you encounter cruel and harsh human sides, you simply CANNOT remain the same. That's what makes any other scene in this anime feel fake. Because I'm looking at a doll, not at a real human being. And I cannot feel catharsis by any means if its a doll crying over her father's book, or apologizing to Cyril, though remaining as weak as she always was.

### Tonal Inconsistency of Humor

It's also interesting to see how the authors manage to both make fun of Monica's social anxiety and make the viewer cry because of the roots of it. You could also mention these drama things being presented by boring flashbacks that sort of lack viewers' involvement, but let's put that aside. The humor is generally fine, but I find it rather cruel to laugh and cry simultaneously because of her tragic past. It's not the same as in Bocchi the Rock or any other representative. It feels different, because Silent Witch shows us main heroine struggling, intentionally tries to make us cry over her past, and then make fun of the result of such a past in the same episode. Sometimes in the very same minute, I believe.

### Final Verdict

The anime is cute, it's beautiful and so on. Yet the main heroine remains weak, while the writers make fun of her... in a way. And when there's not much to laugh about, Monica manages to genuinely shock the viewer by being incredibly morally blind, making the viewer literally feel a wasted potential of how the story could've been better if the heroine had done... something? Besides playing chess, making (fake?) friends and basking in men's attention.
