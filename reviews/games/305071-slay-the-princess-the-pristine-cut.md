## A metaphorical love story
*May 2026*

The game starts with a pretty simple and straightforward exposition: you're in the woods, the princess is in the basement of the cabin, and you have to slay her to let the world exist. It's a pretty bizarre way to start a story, isn't it? I mean, you probably don't expect a game to throw a player into some woods right away without any proper tutorial or at least some introduction besides the aforementioned premise.

When you proceed and spend a few hours there, you find yourself thinking: "What was that even about? They didn't even let me get all the answers despite me playing the entire game from start to finish!". And that's quite fair, I must assume... but let's not be too tedious here. We'd better discuss this game more thoroughly.

### Gameplay and Visuals

What about the gameplay? Yeah, there isn't much to say, is there? It's basically a visual novel. I didn't think about it being one before actually booting up the game and searching up its game engine. But don't you dare underestimate this genre, because it could - and this game definitely does - please the player with a great story and all visuals and words needed to succeed.

The usual Ren'Py visual novel is all about having static backgrounds, sprites, some music, ambient and many, many words. And let's be honest on that, they do their job. Right to this day. If the writers are able to provide a good story while maintaining a moderate level of style in terms of their visuals, people usually like the game. I mean, those who play visual novels at all. It's kind of a niche genre, after all. The other representatives of the genre are the VNs that cost the developers **a lot** of money. Remember all these voice lines in Steins;Gate made by professional seiyuus? They are incredibly expensive. Live2D to make cat ears of the sprites in Nekopara twitch? Expensive.

But it's a little different with "Slay the Princess", right? There is a visual dynamic. If Princess is a ghost or a nightmare, she flickers a lot. If she is The Spectre and needs to go behind your back, you'll see a pretty much animated motion of her getting closer to the screen. The same thing goes for many parts of the game. The Beast lunges at you. It's dynamic. The simplicity of the artstyle makes it feel more devastating, more scary, but also captivating. And you only need to ask Abby Howard to draw the visuals and Tony Howard-Arias to code this into the actual game. And it doesn't involve inviting a massive development team for these things.

The code itself does a pretty nice job as well. For example, the game closes itself when you tell The Shifting Mound you'll be waiting forever instead of doing what she wants.

### The beauty of choices

Okay, so... it seems like we're somewhere deep in the forest, and there is some voice asking us to slay the princess. It doesn't use "would you kindly" nor it says "Stanley went through the **red** door", but it surely has its own charisma and an undeniably charming voice to lead us the way.

When you obediently - or maybe not much - follow the path, you always have different voices accompanying you on your journeys. And they are simply beautiful. Not only they serve to fill the player with different points of view and arguments on the possible actions, but these invisible guys are also quite hilarious in the best way possible. Each represent a different way of looking at things. They could be suspicious, stubborn, paranoid, skeptical, cold, broken and so on. And with all that in mind it's somewhat unbelievable to see some of them being incredibly funny, cynical, logical, sarcastic and stuff. They do also swap each other from cycle to cycle, which makes it easier to stay interested in this specific way of pushing the narrative. It's a decent way to introduce fun into the story without having to draw endless numbers of sprites and actual characters.

The actual ability of every one of us to lead the story as we want it to be is probably one of the most exciting moments about the game. I've heard some video creator comparing this feeling as if a player is a kid in an amusement park with an ability to go anywhere, to choose any route and have fun. That's literally it. You can see the princesses being pretty much just like you would imagine them to be in the corresponding environment.

- If you decide to take the blade, you meet a cold and assertive version of Princess. Let's say, you see her and you decide to talk to her and save her afterwards, resisting the urge of the narrator to interfere with your desires. You're being skeptical about the narrator's motives, about the princess' true powers and so much on. In the result you get The Prisoner - which is basically a skeptical princess trying to defend her heart by being moderately paranoid - and, well, a "Voice of the Skeptic". Who would've thought.
- If you decide to not take the blade and instead try to save the princess while resisting the narrator, you get The Damsel. The surroundings change, the cabin doesn't look half as bad as before, and the basement gives out the basic "hero saves a princess" vibe. We get the "Voice of the Smitten", which chats all about his love toward the princess and his power of feelings and stuff, almost never shutting up. That's hilarious, but also absolutely reasonable. What else could we get when we do our best to save a tender and fragile princess?

All the scenarios are unexpectedly logical and rational if you reflect on them afterwards. If you kill the princess without hesitating, you could either wonder if she's alive (doubting her death) or just leave (being sure she's dead). These decisions lead you to the totally different paths. Every branch is literally a psychological echo of your own choices. But that's not the only pleasant thing about all the story development. The first starting screen says: "This is a love story". Every action of yours shapes your character into someone well-versed in how to interact with Princess. And, meanwhile, The Shifting Mound gets a vessel that's basically serves the same purpose - it helps her to shape herself. Some routes even let you hear interactions between the narrator and the princess, while the others shows you the picture of the world from princess' point of view. These perspective changes makes the story stay captivating, engaging and interesting to the player.

If we drop that metaphorical wrapping around the characters, they pretty much resemble how actual real life couples build their relationships. They are being stubborn, broken, cheated, smitten, cold, skeptical just like The Long Quiet after dealing with Princess. And she feels all the exact same things as we interact with her. When we think of saving her, but then go upstairs to grab a blade - we raise The Witch. She's gonna betray us just like we did. She won't trust any of our words. But what if that player reflected on his previous actions and really, really changed the way of thinking? What if he really wants to gain some sort of trust and give her the blade? Then he encounters The Thorn. It's probably one of the most beautiful and lovely routes in the entire game. Princess tries to believe us, but then gets betrayed by our decision to grab the blade. When we're really sorry and she doesn't believe that, she regrets killing us afterwards. She feels guilty, and we get an enormous sympathy toward her. That's just like how actual couples work. They make mistakes. They get betrayed. They feel sorry and they get stronger. This is definitely a love story.

Moreover, Princess acts drastically different all the time. It's rather hard to be bored because a player meets more and more versions of her. She could be a creepy ghost or a lovely girl. You could go down the stairs and find yourself in a wild bushy foliage, or in a royal palace. And it's up **to you** to decide how you treat her. The more you play, the more time you spend with her, with different versions of her. That's the emotional connection that blooms through the play cycles as you reflect on the vessels' destiny and the right thing to do in terms of "saving worlds".

### The actual leitmotif

The catch of the game is you don't actually understand anything until after you've met The Echo. There surely is some strange creature that asks you to collect vessels for her and... well, you do what she asks. It's basically the flaw and the strength. And it works, but solely because the developers decided to make the ending appear in just a few hours. That's actually a great thing to do, because an average player wouldn't be too bored, not getting the idea of what's happening to him.

And when you think about saving worlds, you actually start diving into the game even more. It's not just a random girl anymore in the middle of nowhere - it's, well, yeah, an abstract girl in the middle of literal abstract nowhere, but we have the ability to finally confront the philosophy of the antagonist with all - or at least some of - the context in mind.

This is when it starts resembling many different games. But in a good way, I believe. The Long Quiet has the option to confront The Echo with this specific question: "If you want me to destroy the concept of transformation, how is that existence any better than death? Or even different from death at all? Honestly, it feels worse". And that's only fair to ask this question. To yourself, at most.

There was a world where death and birth lived together. If one were to be born, then obviously the other representative of that same species has to die eventually. No one gets to be immortal. Sounds rather familiar, doesn't it? That is literally our Earth. We can't do much to completely defy the very nature, can we? But The Narrator tried to prove it wrong. He made "Construct" and both The Long Quiet and The Shifting Mound. And The Echo gives us the answer: he just wanted deaths to stop. He even killed himself in the sacrifice of raising a world without people witnessing their beloved meeting cruel oblivion.

"Slay the Princess" gives us an utterly interesting topic to examine. Is it okay to just... stagnate? Is it okay for people to die eventually? Isn't such a reality harsh? And the player actually has a choice of either trying to balance between the chaos and the quiet existence, making the world chaotic or making it stay still forever. And it's simply great when the game could be seen as an art, making you think about such deep and important concepts.

Moreover, the actual structure of the story makes the game unusually replayable. You cannot get ALL the answers from The Echo until you give The Shifting Mound these 5 vessels at least 2 or 3 times. And you don't have to listen to the same conversation over and over again, because every new cycle gives you the ability to invest in a brand new vessel with a completely different behavior and a voice accompanying you. That means you might have a natural desire to start over and over to get more pieces of puzzle, which most similar games really lack.

## The flaw and conclusion

The dialogues in "Slay the Princess" are absolutely marvelous. The voices give you so much fun. Thanks to the concept of the game, the princess is able to change her attitude and motives and backstory on a whim while still being moderately reasonable. The visuals are simple yet charming and the music fits just right.

The most disappointing part of the game is probably the radical desire of the authors to dive into these metaphorical concepts. It's somewhat odd to expect people to empathize with the story when the actual backstory is "well, there was someone who was sad of losing beloved ones and he made Construct to save all people from suffering". I mean, yeah, we definitely could suspend the disbelief, because the game stays consistent with that context, but it's definitely not making the entire plot any more emotional. It's not [[1342-beyond-two-souls|Nathan Dawkins]] with all the painful and beautiful motives to save the family. It's not [[305152-clair-obscur-expedition-33|Alicia]] with the... well, painful decisions. We weren't able to see and to feel the backstory of "Slay the Princess". The player was trapped with the beautiful, cynical and really interesting voice throughout the hours, but at the end of the day does the owner of the voice actually give a player any reason to feel some sympathy? We didn't see him suffer. We didn't even hear him say anything with a broken, emotional voice. We aren't invested emotionally at all. All the focus is on the main characters, and they are really interesting, but... it's a little too one-sided for the story. It makes a player less curious about thinking of whether The Echo's motives are fair or not.

But we were able to let both the gods experience each other's flaws and strengths, giving them enough space to make mistakes. To be loved and cheated on, to be skeptical and simply hungry. And it's a crucial part of the story, which actually somewhat eclipses all the flat backstory with a powerful emotional commitment and interesting philosophy. All while being scary, beautiful, completely unrelated and so obviously related to all of us and the real world.
